Course overview

Insight into how human brains work in their most ubiquitous and biologically meaningful context, social interaction, has remained largely elusive. This course ventures into this “dark matter” of social neuroscience, pursuing the question of what constitutes a “meeting of minds”. Conceptual and methodological challenges of studying human interaction are dealt with in-class discussions, laboratories, and small group research projects on selected topics. Students will be expected to design, run, analyze, and write up an interaction study answering a question of their choosing. Example research projects include but are not limited to studies of human interactive behavior in the real world, the lab, simulated scenarios, or on social media.


Course learning goals

  • Gain insight into the “dark matter” of social neuroscience
  • Gain insight into how human brains achieve mutual understanding and how this elusive capacity underpins social interaction, culture, and society
  • Get a thorough background in scientific literature and approaches for advancing our understanding of human interaction
  • Learn how to design, run, analyze, and write up human interaction studies

Lectures

Module I: Defining Dark Matter

Welcome to Experimental Study of Human Interaction, where we will venture into the “dark matter” of social neuroscience, pursuing the question of what constitutes a “meeting of minds”. The course consists of two modules. The first module is theory-oriented and meant to get you up to speed on the latest knowledge on human interaction. For now, I recommend reading the chapter from the late Nicholas Humphrey, “The society of selves”, on the apparent paradox between our exceptional sociability and exceptional loneliness. The short article by Thalia Wheatley and me, “Beyond the isolated brain”, discusses ways by which social neuroscience could take into account both these essential elements of the human condition. The second module is research-oriented and provides you with an opportunity to extend knowledge on human interaction in the form a principled research project. As do most research projects, part of your project will rely on programming to analyze and visualize data. Today, two similar coding languages are popular in academia, MATLAB and Python. Starting week 2, we will use these interchangeably throughout the course. The first, MATLAB, comes with its own interface. For the second, Python, a popular format is Jupyter Notebook, an interactive platform that runs locally in the web browser. Google’s Colab constitutes a Google Docs-like spin-off of this platform. It runs entirely in the cloud, does not require a setup, and can be shared with people and groups. I recommend you start familiarizing yourself with all three platforms by going through the installation and introduction links provided. Just like how we really learn to handle a car after obtaining our drivers license, the goal here is to get yourself to a level where you “get by” handling and plotting your data.
